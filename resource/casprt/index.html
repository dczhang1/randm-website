Calculated and Spontaneous Risk-Taking Scale (CASPRT)

The CASPRT is a novel psychometric instrument that measures individual differences in risk-taking styles. Drawing from dual-process theories of decision making, it distinguishes between two approaches to risk:

Calculated Risk-Taking

The tendency to take risks after deliberate consideration of potential outcomes, with careful weighing of benefits against costs.

Spontaneous Risk-Taking

The tendency to take risks based on feelings or impulses, often without fully considering potential consequences.

Our research shows that these risk styles differently predict important outcomes across work, school, and life domains.

Key Findings

  • Calculated and spontaneous risk-taking are moderately correlated but distinct
  • Calculated risk-takers show more situation-specific risk attitudes
  • Calculated risk-taking predicts adaptive outcomes (creativity, entrepreneurship)
  • Spontaneous risk-taking predicts maladaptive behaviors (crime, safety violations)
  • The scale is measurement invariant across age and sex
