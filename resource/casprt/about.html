About the Scale

The Calculated and Spontaneous Risk-Taking Scale (CASPRT) is a 12-item measure developed and validated across seven studies with over 2,100 participants.

Scale Items

Calculated Risk-Taking

  1. I tend to take calculated risks in my everyday life
  2. I am willing to take risks after careful consideration of the potential outcomes
  3. I consider myself a calculated risk taker
  4. I believe taking calculated risks is important to achieve your goals
  5. I enjoy taking risks that involve calculation
  6. My friends would consider me as someone that takes calculated risks

Spontaneous Risk-Taking

  1. I enjoy the thrill of taking risks without thinking
  2. I am attracted to risky activities where I don't know what will happen
  3. I don't think much about the consequences of risky decisions. I just make them
  4. I find it difficult to resist the urge to take risks
  5. I am a spontaneous risk taker
  6. I often jump into risky situations without thinking through them

Scoring Instructions

Each item is rated on a 5-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree to 5 = strongly agree). Subscale scores are calculated by averaging responses to the six items in each dimension.

Response Score
Strongly Disagree 1
Disagree 2
Neither Agree nor Disagree 3
Agree 4
Strongly Agree 5

Scale Development Process

The CASPRT was developed through a rigorous process:

Phase 1: Item Generation

  • Initial seed items generated by researchers
  • Items reviewed for content validity
  • 34 initial items reduced to 12

Phase 2: Psychometric Testing

  • Exploratory and confirmatory factor analyses
  • Item Response Theory analysis
  • Test-retest reliability assessment

Phase 3: Validation

  • Convergent and discriminant validity
  • Predictive validity across multiple domains
  • Measurement invariance testing

Theoretical Foundation

The CASPRT is grounded in dual-process theories of thinking and decision-making, which distinguish between intuitive (quick, automatic, feeling-based) and analytical (slower, effortful, deliberative) processes. While this framework has been applied to risk perception and decision-making, existing psychometric measures had not captured these distinct approaches to risk-taking.

Intuitive Process

  • Quick and automatic
  • Driven by feelings and affect
  • Little conscious effort required
  • Holistic processing

Analytical Process

  • Slow and deliberate
  • Systematic evaluation of options
  • Requires conscious effort
  • Rule-based processing

Our research shows that risk-taking styles reflect these dual processes, with calculated risk-taking being associated with analytical processing and spontaneous risk-taking being associated with intuitive processing.
