Contextualized Economic Preference Module

The Contextualized Economic Preference Module is a comprehensive survey instrument designed to measure three fundamental economic preferences in workplace contexts: time preferences (patience), risk preferences, and social preferences (including altruism, positive reciprocity, and negative reciprocity).

This module adapts established economic preference measures by contextualizing them to workplace scenarios, making them more relevant and meaningful for organizational research while maintaining their theoretical foundations.

Module Overview

The Contextualized Economic Preference Module consists of three main components:

  1. Time Preferences (Patience): A sequence of choices between smaller, immediate rewards and larger, delayed rewards in workplace contexts.
  2. Risk Preferences: A sequence of choices between guaranteed rewards and probabilistic rewards with higher expected values in workplace contexts.
  3. Social Preferences: A combination of self-assessment questions and hypothetical situations measuring altruism, positive reciprocity, and negative reciprocity in workplace settings.

Time Preferences (Patience)

This component measures an individual's willingness to delay gratification for larger future rewards in workplace contexts.

Structure

The patience measure uses a staircase procedure with binary choices between:

  • A smaller reward available immediately (fixed at $1,000)
  • A larger reward available after a 12-month delay (varies based on previous choices)

Sample Items

"Please consider the following: Would you rather take on a work project that gives you a bonus of $1,000 today, or a different project that gives you $1,540 in 12 months?"

Adaptive Structure

Based on a participant's response, the next question adjusts the future reward amount. If the participant chooses the immediate reward, the future reward is increased; if they choose the delayed reward, the future reward is decreased. This adaptive design efficiently identifies the participant's indifference point.

Self-Assessment Component

"How willing are you to give up something at work that is beneficial for you today in order to benefit more from that in the future?"

Risk Preferences

This component measures an individual's attitude toward risk in workplace decision-making contexts.

Structure

The risk measure uses a staircase procedure with binary choices between:

  • A guaranteed work bonus of a specific amount
  • A 50:50 chance of receiving a larger bonus ($3,000) or nothing

Sample Items

"What would you prefer: to take on a work project, where the bonus is contingent on its success, with a 50 percent chance of earning a performance bonus of $3,000 and a 50 percent chance of earning nothing, or to take on a work project with an assured bonus of $1,600?"

Adaptive Structure

Similar to the patience measure, the risk measure adapts subsequent questions based on previous responses to efficiently identify the participant's risk premium.

Self-Assessment Component

"In general, how willing or unwilling you are to take risks at work?"

Social Preferences

This component measures three facets of social preferences in workplace contexts.

Altruism

Measures willingness to benefit others without personal gain.

Hypothetical Situation

"Imagine the following situation: You unexpectedly receive $1,000 bonus from your company. There's an office charity drive for the Employee Assistance Program to support coworkers in need. How much of your bonus would you donate to the program?"

Self-Assessment

"How willing are you to give to good causes at work without expecting anything in return?"
