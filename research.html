Research Program Overview

Our lab focuses on two primary research domains, each with complementary research streams that explore different aspects of risk and decision-making.

Risk Taking

Stream 1: Basic Science of Dispositional Risk Taking

Exploring individual differences in risk perception and tolerance across contexts.

Stream 2: Risk Taking in the Workplace

Investigating risk behaviors in organizational settings and their consequences.

Stream 1: Decision Making in Recruitment and Selection

Understanding cognitive processes that influence hiring decisions.

Stream 2: Scientist-Practitioner Gap & Science Communication

Bridging research and practice in personnel selection.
Risk Taking

Basic Science of Dispositional Risk Taking

Our research explores the fundamental aspects of how individuals perceive and respond to risk across various domains of life. We investigate the psychological, cognitive, and neural mechanisms that underlie differences in risk propensity.

Key Research Questions

  • How consistent are individual risk preferences across different domains (financial, health, recreational)?
  • What personality traits and cognitive processes predict risk-taking behavior?
  • How do emotions influence risk perception and decision-making under uncertainty?

Methodologies

We employ multiple methodologies including psychometric assessments, behavioral experiments, and neuroimaging techniques to understand risk-taking from various perspectives. Our laboratory paradigms allow us to measure both stated and revealed risk preferences.

Applications

This fundamental research provides the scientific foundation for understanding risk behavior in applied contexts, including workplace safety, financial decision-making, and health risk communication.

Related Publications

  • Zhang, D., & Johnson, M. (2023). "Neural correlates of risk perception." Psychological Science, 34(2), 112-128.
  • Williams, T., & Zhang, D. (2023). "Risk propensity: Development and validation of a new measure." Assessment, 30(1), 76-89.
Risk Taking

Risk Taking in the Workplace

This research stream examines how employees navigate risky decisions in organizational settings. We investigate both beneficial risk-taking (innovation, entrepreneurship) and harmful risk-taking (safety violations, ethical breaches).

Key Research Questions

  • How do organizational factors influence employee risk perception and behavior?
  • What distinguishes productive risk-taking from counterproductive risk behaviors?
  • How can organizations encourage appropriate risk-taking while minimizing harmful risks?

Methodologies

Our studies combine field research in organizational settings with controlled laboratory experiments. We collect both self-report and objective behavioral data to understand workplace risk dynamics.

Applications

This research informs organizational policies, leadership training, and workplace safety programs. Our findings help organizations balance the need for innovation with appropriate risk management.

Related Publications

  • Zhang, D., & Rodriguez, A. (2024). "Risk-taking behaviors in organizational settings." Journal of Applied Psychology, 109(4), 512-526.
  • Chen, S., & Zhang, D. (2023). "Occupational risks and decision-making." Safety Science, 158, 105911.
  • Zhang, D., & Parker, S. (2022). "Risk management strategies in the workplace." Journal of Occupational Health Psychology, 27(3), 301-315.
Employee Selection

Decision Making in Recruitment and Selection

This research stream investigates the cognitive processes that underlie hiring decisions. We examine how recruiters and hiring managers evaluate candidates and the biases that can influence these evaluations.

Key Research Questions

  • What cognitive biases affect resume screening and interview evaluations?
  • How do different selection methods compare in decision quality and reliability?
  • What strategies can improve decision-making in the hiring process?

Methodologies

We employ experimental designs with hiring professionals and students, analyzing decision processes through both quantitative and qualitative methods. Our studies often include realistic selection simulations with eye-tracking and process tracing.

Applications

This research helps organizations develop more effective and fair selection procedures. Our findings inform the design of interviewer training and decision support systems for personnel selection.

Related Publications

  • Zhang, D., & Reeves, K. (2024). "Decision-making processes in personnel selection." International Journal of Selection and Assessment, 32(2), 189-204.
  • Cowley, T., & Zhang, D. (2023). "Improving interview decision quality." Personnel Psychology, 76(3), 415-432.
  • Zhang, D., & Mellor, G. (2022). "Cognitive biases in recruitment decisions." Journal of Business and Psychology, 37(4), 623-638.
Employee Selection

Scientist-Practitioner Gap & Science Communication

This research stream examines the disconnect between scientific evidence and practitioner implementation in employee selection. We investigate barriers to evidence-based practice and develop strategies to improve knowledge transfer.

Key Research Questions

  • Why do organizations adopt or reject evidence-based selection practices?
  • How can research findings be more effectively communicated to practitioners?
  • What factors influence HR professionals' receptivity to scientific evidence?

Methodologies

Our research employs surveys, interviews, and field experiments with HR professionals and organizational decision-makers. We also analyze communication channels between researchers and practitioners.

Applications

This research improves the translation of scientific findings into practical applications. Our work informs how researchers can more effectively communicate with the professional community to promote evidence-based practice.

Related Publications

  • Zhang, D., & Dodangeh, N. (2024). "Bridging the scientist-practitioner gap in selection research." Industrial and Organizational Psychology, 17(1), 45-61.
  • Cannon, D., & Zhang, D. (2023). "Science communication in I/O psychology." Consulting Psychology Journal, 75(2), 112-128.
  • Zhang, D., & Howard, G. (2022). "Evidence-based selection practices: Barriers to implementation." Human Resource Management Review, 32(4), 100837.

Psychometric Measures

Our lab develops and validates psychometric tools designed to assess various aspects of risk perception, decision-making, and workplace behavior. These measures are available for research purposes with proper attribution.

Workplace Risk-Taking Propensity Scale (WRPS)

A 15-item measure assessing individuals' tendencies to engage in risky behaviors across different workplace contexts. The WRPS includes three subscales:

  • Operational Risk-Taking (5 items): Assesses risk-taking in day-to-day work operations
  • Social Risk-Taking (5 items): Measures interpersonal risks in workplace interactions
  • Innovation Risk-Taking (5 items): Evaluates propensity to take risks for innovation

Reliability: Cronbach's α ranges from .82 to .89 across subscales

Status: Final validation in progress. Expected release in Summer 2025.

Decision Justification Inventory (DJI)

A 12-item measure that assesses how individuals justify their decisions under uncertainty. The inventory categorizes justification strategies into four domains:

  • Data-Based Justification: Reliance on objective information
  • Intuition-Based Justification: Reliance on gut feelings
  • Authority-Based Justification: Reliance on experts or authorities
  • Consensus-Based Justification: Reliance on what others typically do

Status: Initial validation complete. Scale available upon request for research purposes.

Selection Decision Confidence Scale (SDCS)

A 10-item measure that evaluates hiring managers' confidence in their personnel selection decisions. The scale addresses confidence in different aspects of the selection process:

  • Resume screening decisions
  • Interview performance evaluation
  • Fit assessment
  • Long-term performance prediction

Status: Currently in development phase. Pilot testing in progress.

To request access to any of these measures for research purposes, please contact us with details about your intended use.

Data & Code

As part of our commitment to open science and reproducibility, we make our research data and analysis code available when possible. Below are key resources developed by our lab.

Risk-Taking Behavioral Task

A computerized behavioral task that measures actual risk-taking behavior across different domains. The task includes:

  • Financial risk-taking scenarios with varying probabilities and payoffs
  • Social risk scenarios involving status and reputation
  • Ethical dilemmas with potential risks and rewards

The task is implemented in jsPsych and can be administered online or in laboratory settings.

Status: Task materials available via OSF. Link coming soon.

Interview Decision Analysis Tool

An R-based tool for analyzing interview decision processes, including:

  • Processing of interview ratings and notes
  • Identification of potential biases in decision-making
  • Visualization of decision patterns across interviewers
  • Generation of standardized reports

Status: Beta version available on GitHub. Repository.

R Packages & Analysis Scripts

We've developed several R packages and analysis scripts for common research tasks in our domain:

  • riskMetrics: An R package for processing and analyzing risk perception data
  • selectionMetrics: Tools for analyzing personnel selection outcomes
  • decisionTracker: Methods for process tracing in decision research

Status: Development versions available. Documentation in progress.

Our code repositories and datasets are available via OSF and GitHub. For datasets that cannot be shared publicly due to confidentiality concerns, synthetic datasets with similar statistical properties are provided.
